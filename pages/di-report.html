Reflecting the American People

Too often, outdated tools, systems, and practices make interacting with the government cumbersome and frustrating. President Barack Obama founded the U.S. Digital Service in August 2014, bringing together the best engineering and design talent and changing our government’s approach to technology.

Since then, hundreds of mission-driven professionals have completed tours of service, furthering the USDS mission to deliver better government services to the American people through technology and design. We’ve grown from less than a dozen staffers to more than 200 today.

One of our goals is to build a workforce that reflects the myriad identities of the people we serve. We believe that fostering diversity, inclusion, equity, and accessibility is vital to our ability to effectively collaborate with public servants to imagine and build digital solutions to solve everyday challenges people face.

A letter from leadership

The people we serve are why we’re here — improving their lives and opportunities is at the center of everything we do. We know and have seen that we better serve the public when our staff represents the country’s diversity. We’ve witnessed first-hand that having varied lived experiences — from growing up in a bilingual home, to living with a disability, to earning the first college degree in your family — allows us to address the diverse needs of our nation more equitably, inclusively, and accessibly. Creating an inclusive and equitable culture that attracts talented, mission-driven staff from different backgrounds is foundational to one of our values: “Hire and empower great people.”

To do this well requires honest self-examination and intentional, iterative changes. We set out to better understand our organization as it exists now — including our race and ethnicity, gender, age, LGBTQ+ identities, and disability status — through a voluntary, self-reported survey. Eighty-nine percent of full-time USDSers shared their demographic backgrounds and identities. Our team included diversity, equity, inclusion, and accessibility (DEIA) practitioners, data scientists, and privacy experts — and we carefully designed a staff survey that would accurately document the breadth of our identities while protecting individuals’ information. The analysis of that data is the basis of the report below.

We’re pleased to provide this information as a mechanism for transparency and public accountability as we carry out our important public service mission. Additionally, we’re sharing some details on how we partner with agencies across the Federal Government on project work, grounded in our mission to serve millions of people from different backgrounds.

We know that this is a journey, and a critically important one. We’re deeply committed to investing in DEIA at USDS, and have taken some key steps — one of which is the creation of this report so that we can continue to evaluate ourselves — and other steps which are outlined below. We work to build momentum by taking an iterative and learning approach in all that we do. In that same vein, we look forward to evolving as an organization as we continue making intentional progress on this journey.

Creating a More Inclusive Culture

One of our core values is to “Optimize for results, not optics,” and we’ve made some big changes to move towards a more inclusive workforce that matches the diversity of America.

This work is imperative to us: We’ve seen the incredible value that teams with diversity in age, race and ethnicity, gender, and other factors bring to our work, both in partnership with agencies across the Federal Government and within our own team at USDS.

Since our first Diversity and Inclusion report in 2020, we’ve:

  • Hired a full-time Diversity, Equity, Inclusion, and Accessibility (DEIA) Director. This is a leadership position reporting directly to our Administrator.
  • Established a DEIA Council with staff who represent different races, ethnicities, and disciplines within USDS, including underrepresented identities.
  • Focused our recruitment events strategy on finding underrepresented technologists. We’ve participated in conferences hosted by diverse organizations, such as AfroTech, Lesbians Who Tech, and Techqueria, among other groups, to connect with Black, LGBTQ+, and Latinx people, respectively.
  • Worked with our agency partners to enable content translation across a wide variety of languages.
  • Improved our ability to attract more diverse participants for user research in our project work.
  • Began creating safe spaces for USDSers to connect to DEIA initiatives with DEIA Office Hours and small group discussions.
  • Launched annual, optional surveys to measure employee demographics, engagement, and belonging.
  • Prioritized cultural observances and celebrations, internally and externally. This year, we’ve highlighted the experiences of Asian American and Pacific Islander, Black, Hispanic, and LGBTQ+ people, and women at USDS.
  • Offered voluntary training and presentations to educate staff on specific cultures and identities.
  • Designed a strategy to continually measure and iterate on our DEIA goals.

The future of DEIA at USDS

As we look to the year ahead, we'll continue to embed DEIA principles in everything we do, with a focus on:

  • Attracting diverse talent who reflect the populations we serve.
  • Collaboratively creating an inclusive workplace where all USDSers feel seen, heard, valued, respected, and engaged.
  • Leveraging the diverse backgrounds, lived experiences, skills, and passions of USDSers to consistently deliver inclusive, equitable, and accessible products and services to the public.
  • Continuously iterating on our process, building upon what works, changing what doesn’t, and incorporating new ideas as we go.
